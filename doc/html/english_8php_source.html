478 
479 <h2>General</h2>
480 <p>
481 In rugby, a scrum refers to the manner of restarting the game after a minor infraction. In the early 1990s,
482 Ken Schwaber used what would become Scrum at his company, Advanced Development Methods, and Jeff Sutherland,
483 with John Scumniotales and Jeff McKenna, developed a similar approach at Easel Corporation, and were the
484 first to refer to it using the single word Scrum.
485 </p>
486 
487 <h2>Sprint</h2>
488 <p>
489 A sprint is the basic unit of development in Scrum. Sprints last between one week and one month, and
490 are a "timeboxed" (i.e. restricted to a specific duration) effort of a constant length. Each sprint is
491 preceded by a planning meeting, where the tasks for the sprint are identified and an estimated
492 commitment for the sprint goal is made, and followed by a review or retrospective meeting, where the
493 progress is reviewed and lessons for the next sprint are identified. During each sprint, the team
494 creates finished portions of a product. The set of features that go into a sprint come from the
495 product backlog, which is a prioritized list of requirements. Which backlog items go into the sprint
496 is determined during the sprint planning meeting.
497 </p>
498 
499 <p>During this meeting, the Product Owner informs the
500 team of the items in the product backlog that he or she wants completed (the ones with the highest
501 priority). The team then determines how much of this they can commit to complete during the next
502 sprint, and records this in the sprint backlog. During a sprint, no one is allowed to change the
503 sprint backlog, which means that the requirements are frozen for that sprint. Development is timeboxed
504 such that the sprint must end on time; if requirements are not completed for any reason they are left
505 out and returned to the product backlog. After a sprint is completed, the team demonstrates how to
506 use the software.
507 </p>
508 
509 <h2>Roles</h2>
510 
511 <p>
512 <b>Roles</b>&nbsp;
513 Scrum teams consist of three core roles and a range of ancillary roles core roles are often referred to as
514 pigs and ancillary roles as chickens (after the story The Chicken and the Pig). The core roles in Scrum
515 teams are those committed to the project in the Scrum process they are the ones producing the product
516 (objective of the project).
517 </p>
518 
519 <p>
520 <b>Product Owner</b>&nbsp;
521 The Product Owner represents the voice of the customer and is accountable for ensuring that the team delivers
522 value to the business. The Product Owner writes customer-centric items (typically user stories), prioritizes
523 them, and adds them to the product backlog. Scrum teams should have one Product Owner, and while they may
524 also be a member of the development team, it is recommended that this role not be combined with that of
525 Scrum Master.
526 </p>
527 
528 <p>
529 <b>Development Team</b>&nbsp;
530 The Development Team is responsible for delivering potentially shippable product increments at the end of each
531 Sprint. A Development Team is made up of 3 a 9 people with cross functional skills who do the actual work
532 (analyse, design, develop, test, technical communication, document, etc.). The Development Team in Scrum is
533 self-organizing, even though they may interface with project management organizations (PMOs).
534 </p>
535 
536 <p>
537 <b>Scrum Master</b>&nbsp;
538 Scrum is facilitated by a Scrum Master, sometimes written as ScrumMaster, who is accountable for removing
539 impediments to the ability of the team to deliver the sprint goal/deliverables. The Scrum Master is not the
540 team leader, but acts as a buffer between the team and any distracting influences. The Scrum Master ensures
541 that the Scrum process is used as intended. The Scrum Master is the enforcer of rules. A key part of the
